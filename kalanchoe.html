Kalanchoe — nicknamed Flaming Katy — is the world's most gifted succulent flowering plant. Beloved for its dense clusters of waxy, jewel-bright flowers that last for six to eight weeks without any fuss, it thrives on bright light and minimal watering. Originally from Madagascar, it has conquered windowsills and indoor gardens globally with its extraordinary resilience and vivid colour range. Available in sixteen spectacular named varieties spanning purple, yellow, pink, orange, red, white, and bicolour forms.

Short Days Trigger Reblooming
Kalanchoe is a short-day plant — exposing it to 14 hours of darkness daily for 6 weeks tricks it into setting new flower buds. This simple technique allows you to rebloom your Kalanchoe repeatedly throughout the year.

My Superpowers
Blooms for 6–8 Weeks
Kalanchoe's waxy flower clusters last far longer than most flowering plants — a single flush provides weeks of colour with no effort.
Needs Almost No Water
As a succulent, Kalanchoe stores water in its thick leaves. Water once every 1–2 weeks — perfect for forgetful or busy plant owners.

Psst — secrets
Did You Know?
Named After a Chinese Word
The word Kalanchoe comes from the Cantonese "Kalan Chauhuy" meaning "that which falls and grows" — a reference to how fallen leaves root themselves and sprout new plants spontaneously.
Sent to Space
Kalanchoe was one of the first plants sent aboard a Soviet space station in the 1970s — scientists studied its ability to bloom in zero gravity as part of early space botany research.
Medicinal Powerhouse
In traditional African and Brazilian medicine, Kalanchoe has been used to treat infections, inflammation, and fever for centuries. Modern research confirms several bioactive compounds with antibacterial properties.
A True Succulent
Unlike most flowering plants, Kalanchoe stores water in its thick, fleshy leaves — this is why it survives two weeks without watering and thrives in the driest indoor environments.
Over 200 Wild Species
The Kalanchoe genus contains over 200 wild species native to Madagascar and tropical Africa — the vast majority are still unknown to most gardeners, with extraordinary forms yet to be cultivated.
