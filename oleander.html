Evergreen ShrubMediterranean OriginDrought HardyLong BloomerLandscape Statement

Oleander (Nerium oleander) is one of the most spectacular flowering shrubs in the world — beloved from the Mediterranean coast to tropical gardens across Asia for its bold, showy flower clusters and tough, resilient nature. With dark, leathery lance-shaped leaves and dense flower heads in shades from crimson red to soft pink, white, peach, and yellow, it brings unmatched drama to hedges, borders, courtyards, and large containers. Originally from the dry riverbanks of the Mediterranean and Southwest Asia, Oleander thrives in heat, tolerates drought, handles coastal winds, and blooms prolifically from spring through autumn. A true architectural plant that makes an immediate statement.

Beautiful but Toxic — Handle with Care
Every part of the Oleander plant is highly toxic if ingested — leaves, flowers, stems, and even the sap. Despite this, it is perfectly safe to grow and touch with normal precautions. Always wash hands after handling, keep away from children and pets, and never burn pruned material. Its toxicity actually helps it resist pests naturally.

How do I thrive?
Essential Care Guide
Sunlight

Oleander demands full sun — a minimum of 6 hours daily is essential for vigorous blooming. In partial shade, flowering becomes sparse and the plant grows leggy. South or west-facing positions are ideal. It performs best in open, exposed locations and genuinely thrives in the intense heat of Indian summers that would stress most other plants.

Watering

One of the most drought-tolerant flowering shrubs available. Established plants in the ground can survive on rainfall alone in most climates. Container plants need watering when the top 2–3 inches of soil are dry — typically every 5–7 days in summer. Oleander hates waterlogged roots; always ensure excellent drainage. Overwatering is far more dangerous than underwatering.

Fertilising

Feed with a balanced slow-release fertiliser in early spring when new growth begins. During the blooming season (May–September), supplement with a high-potassium liquid feed every 3–4 weeks to sustain the continuous flower flush. Avoid excessive nitrogen, which promotes lush foliage at the expense of flowers. A single spring application of well-composted organic matter around the base is highly beneficial.

Pruning

Prune in late winter or very early spring before new growth appears — never during the blooming season as Oleander blooms on new growth. Cut back by up to one-third to maintain a compact, bushy shape and encourage a stronger bloom flush. Remove crossing or dead branches entirely. Always wear gloves when pruning — the milky sap is an irritant and toxic. Never burn pruned material.

Soil & Potting

Well-draining soil is non-negotiable. In the ground, amend heavy clay soils with coarse sand and organic matter. In pots, use a coarse potting mix with added perlite or grit. Terracotta pots are ideal as they allow the soil to breathe. Repot container plants every 2–3 years or when roots begin emerging from drainage holes. Oleander is adaptable to mildly alkaline and saline soils — making it excellent for coastal gardens.

Important to know
Safety & Toxicity
Every Part of Oleander is Toxic
Oleander contains cardiac glycosides throughout every part of the plant. While it is perfectly safe to grow and admire, it must be handled with awareness and kept away from children and pets.
Do: Always Wear Gloves
When pruning, deadheading, or handling any part of the plant, wear protective gloves. Wash hands thoroughly after any contact. The milky sap can cause skin irritation in sensitive individuals.
Do: Keep Away from Children & Pets
Plant in locations not accessible to young children or grazing animals. Ingestion of even small amounts can cause serious illness. Cats, dogs, horses, and cattle are particularly vulnerable.
Do: Dispose of Prunings Safely
Never burn Oleander prunings — the smoke is toxic. Place all cut material in sealed bags for municipal green waste collection. Do not compost Oleander trimmings in home compost bins.
The Upside: Near-Zero Pest Problems
Oleander's natural toxicity makes it almost completely resistant to deer browsing, rabbits, and most common garden pests. It rarely needs pesticide treatment — a genuine advantage in low-maintenance landscapes.
Why do I matter?
My Superpowers
Months of Colour
Oleander blooms continuously from late spring through autumn — providing up to 6 months of spectacular flower display without a break.
Extreme Heat Hardy
Thrives where most flowering plants fail — intense summer heat, drought, coastal salt winds, and poor soils present no challenge to a healthy Oleander.
Natural Privacy Screen
Oleander grows dense and tall — up to 4–6 metres — making it the ideal natural hedge, windbreak, or privacy screen that also flowers beautifully.
Aromatic Blooms
Many Oleander varieties are sweetly fragrant, especially in the evening hours — filling courtyards and terraces with a soft, honey-like scent.
Architectural Beauty
Few plants match the Oleander's ability to create a bold structural statement — trained as a standard tree, clipped hedge, or specimen shrub, it is always magnificent.
Attracts Pollinators
Oleander's colourful blooms draw butterflies, hoverflies, and bees in large numbers — transforming any garden into a lively ecosystem during the bloom season.
Psst — secrets
Did You Know?
Ancient Origins
Oleander has been cultivated for over 2,000 years — it appears in ancient Greek manuscripts, Roman frescoes, and was found preserved in Pompeii. It is one of humanity's oldest ornamental plants.
Survives on Dried Riverbeds
In its native habitat, Oleander grows along completely dry seasonal watercourses where soil moisture is near zero for months. This is why it is among the world's most drought-tolerant flowering shrubs.
Survived the Hiroshima Bombing
Oleander was the first plant to bloom in Hiroshima after the atomic bomb in 1945 — a symbol of recovery so profound that it became the city's official flower, a tribute to resilience and renewal.
Used in Traditional Medicine
Despite its toxicity, Oleander has been used for centuries in Ayurvedic and traditional medicine for specific cardiac conditions. Modern research is studying its compounds for potential cancer-fighting properties.
Over 400 Named Cultivars
From the original wild species, horticulturalists have developed more than 400 named cultivars — spanning single and double flowers in red, pink, white, peach, yellow, salmon, and rare bicolour forms.
