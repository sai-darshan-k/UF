Chrysanthemum Multiflora — the "many-flowered mum" — is the premier autumn garden plant. Unlike standard chrysanthemums, the Multiflora type forms a naturally neat, dome-shaped mound that covers itself in hundreds of small, perfectly formed flowers from October through February. Cultivated for over 2,500 years across East Asia, it has become one of humanity's most revered flowers, symbolising longevity and noble character. Available in 18 spectacular varieties spanning warm pinks, vibrant oranges, golden yellows, pure whites, and regal purples.

Pinching Creates Hundreds More Flowers
Pinching the growing tips in May and June forces the plant to branch aggressively. Each pinched tip produces 3–5 new branches, each ending in a flower cluster — a single pinching operation can double or triple the total number of autumn blooms on each plant.

My Superpowers
Explosive Bloom Density
Hundreds of perfectly formed flowers smother the plant — no foliage visible at peak bloom. Nothing in the garden rivals Multiflora's sheer flower density.
Natural Dome Shape
Grows into a perfect mound without staking or training — the compact Multiflora type maintains its tidy dome-shaped form entirely on its own.

Psst — secrets
Did You Know?
2,500 Years of Cultivation
Chrysanthemum has been cultivated in China since at least 500 BC. The city of Chu-Hsien was literally named "Chrysanthemum City" in antiquity — one of the oldest ornamental plants known to humanity.
Japan's Imperial Seal
The Chrysanthemum is the official seal of the Emperor of Japan. The 16-petal golden chrysanthemum appears on Japanese passports and the throne itself is called the "Chrysanthemum Throne."
Edible Flowers
Chrysanthemum flowers are completely edible — used in Chinese cuisine to make chrysanthemum tea, added to soups, and eaten as salad greens. Flavour ranges from mild-floral to gently bitter.
Natural Air Purifier
NASA's Clean Air Study found Chrysanthemum removes benzene, formaldehyde, and ammonia from indoor air — one of the most effective air-purifying plants ever studied.
Short-Day Flowering
Chrysanthemum is a photoperiodic plant — it only forms flower buds when days become shorter than nights. This is why it blooms naturally in autumn, perfectly timed with shortening October days.
