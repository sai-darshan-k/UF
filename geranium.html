Tender Perennial South African Origin Fragrant Foliage Continuous Bloomer 10 Varieties

Geranium — technically Pelargonium — is one of the most widely grown flowering plants on earth. Beloved for its cheerful rounded flower clusters, aromatic foliage, and extraordinary colour range, it thrives in pots, balconies, garden beds, and window boxes alike. Originally from South Africa, this tough yet beautiful plant blooms nearly year-round in warm climates, rewarding minimal care with generous cascades of colour. Available in ten spectacular varieties.

Deadheading Doubles Blooms
Removing spent flower heads — a practice called deadheading — tricks the Geranium into continuous blooming. The plant redirects energy from seed production into creating fresh flower clusters, resulting in up to double the number of blooms over a season.

Psst — secrets
Did You Know?
Not a True Geranium
The beloved "Geranium" of gardens is actually Pelargonium, a separate genus. True Geraniums are hardy perennials native to Europe. The mix-up dates back to the 18th century when both were classified together — and the name stuck forever.
African Origins
Geranium (Pelargonium) originates from the Cape of Good Hope in South Africa, where over 270 species grow wild. It was introduced to Europe in the 17th century by Dutch traders and quickly became one of the continent's most treasured ornamental plants.
The Perfume Plant
Geranium oil — distilled from the leaves of Pelargonium graveolens — is one of the most important ingredients in perfumery. It is used as a rose substitute in countless fragrances and is grown commercially on a massive scale in Egypt, China, and South Africa.
Natural Mosquito Repellent
Citronella Geranium varieties repel mosquitoes naturally through the compounds in their leaves. Rubbing a leaf releases the repellent oils. Growing them near windows, doors, or seating areas creates a fragrant barrier that insects strongly dislike.
Edible and Medicinal
Geranium flowers and leaves are entirely edible and used to flavour sugars, jellies, teas, and cakes. In traditional medicine, Geranium has been used for centuries to treat wounds, skin conditions, and stress — and modern research has validated several of these properties.
