Chrysanthemum is one of humanity's most cherished flowering plants — cultivated for over 3,000 years in China and Japan, celebrated in festivals, sacred in temples, and the source of one of the most diverse colour palettes in the plant kingdom. A short-day plant, it blooms when nights grow longer, filling autumn and winter gardens with an explosion of yellow, orange, white, pink, red, and purple. We offer twenty exquisite named varieties — each with its own character, bloom form, and glory.

Pinching Creates Bushier Plants
Pinching the growing tip of a young Chrysanthemum forces lateral branching, producing a bushy multi-stemmed plant with far more flower buds. A single pinch in early growth can triple the number of blooms at flowering time — the single most effective technique for maximum display.

Psst — secrets
Did You Know?
3,000 Years of History
Chrysanthemum was first cultivated in China around 1500 BCE. It arrived in Japan in the 8th century CE and became the symbol of the Imperial family — the throne itself is called the "Chrysanthemum Throne."
Japan's National Flower
The Chrysanthemum is the official seal of the Japanese Emperor and appears on the Japanese passport. It is the only plant to be used as a national symbol on a sovereign document.
Natural Pesticide Source
Chrysanthemum flowers are the source of pyrethrin — one of the world's most widely used natural insecticides, extracted from dried flower heads and used in organic pest control globally.
Edible in Asian Cuisine
Chrysanthemum leaves and petals are eaten as vegetables in China, Japan, and Korea — stir-fried, added to soups, and used as garnishes. Chrysanthemum tea is consumed for its cooling, health-giving properties.
Short-Day Flowering Trigger
Chrysanthemum blooms only when nights exceed a critical length — a phenomenon called photoperiodism. Commercial growers use blackout curtains to artificially trigger blooming any time of year.
