Tropical Climber South American Origin Semi-Evergreen Paper Bracts 8 Varieties

Bougainvillea is among the most spectacular flowering plants on earth. Named after French explorer Louis Antoine de Bougainville, this vigorous tropical climber is beloved for its explosion of vivid papery bracts — the colourful structures most people mistake for petals. In reality the true flower is a tiny white or cream trumpet nestled within the bracts. It thrives in full sun and warm climates, rewarding minimal water stress with a breathtaking flush of colour that can last months. Available in eight remarkable varieties.

Drought Stress Triggers Colour
Counterintuitively, slightly stressing Bougainvillea by reducing water triggers the most spectacular blooming. Allowing the soil to dry between waterings signals the plant to produce bracts rather than leafy growth — turning mild neglect into a riot of colour.

Why do I matter?
My Superpowers
Loves Full Sun
Unlike most plants, Bougainvillea thrives in intense heat and direct sun — 6+ hours daily produces the most spectacular bract display.
Drought Tolerant
Once established, Bougainvillea survives on minimal water — it is one of the most water-efficient flowering plants for hot climates.
Months of Bloom
A healthy Bougainvillea can bloom continuously for 4 to 6 months — covering walls, pergolas, and fences in unbroken colour.
8 Colour Varieties
From fiery bicolour to soft peach and pure white — eight distinct varieties to suit every garden style and colour palette.
Architectural Beauty
Bougainvillea transforms plain walls, compound gates, pergolas, and fences into living tapestries of cascading colour.
Attracts Pollinators
The vivid bracts attract butterflies, sunbirds, and bees in large numbers — turning your garden into a thriving wildlife habitat.
Psst — secrets
Did You Know?
Not Actually Flowers
The colourful parts of Bougainvillea are not petals — they are modified leaves called bracts. The true flower is the tiny white or yellow trumpet hidden at the centre of the bracts. The bracts serve as a colourful signal to pollinators from a distance.
Named After an Explorer
Bougainvillea was named after Louis Antoine de Bougainville, the French explorer who led the first French circumnavigation of the globe in 1766–1769. His botanist Philibert Commerson discovered the plant in Brazil and named it in his honour.
Thorns Are a Defence Tool
Bougainvillea's sharp curved thorns are not just for climbing — they anchor the plant onto supporting structures as it grows. In gardens, thorny Bougainvillea planted along boundary walls acts as a highly effective natural security barrier.
Stress Triggers Blooming
Bougainvillea blooms most prolifically under mild stress. Reducing water, limiting root space, and ensuring maximum sun exposure signals the plant to shift energy from leaf growth into bract and flower production — turning apparent hardship into breathtaking colour.
National Flower of Many Nations
Bougainvillea is the official flower of several cities and nations including Grenada, Guam, and numerous cities across Southeast Asia. In India it is one of the most widely cultivated ornamental plants and a symbol of vibrant tropical living.
